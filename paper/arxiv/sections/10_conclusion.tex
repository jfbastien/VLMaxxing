\section{Conclusion}

The paper began with a small absurdity: much of the video did not change, and
the model paid anyway. The measured answer is not one single multiplier. Fresh
videos admit modest but real first-pass gains, and those gains obey a
share-limited ceiling that explains the regime and binds cleanly on the core
ceiling checks, including the matched Qwen cross-architecture probe and the
Qwen sparse-ViT boundary point that skips real vision work but fails fidelity.
Same-video
follow-up queries admit much larger multipliers: raw persistent-KV reuse
reaches sub-second latency inside the \(\leq 16\)f tested envelope, while
selective re-prefill repairs the deeper cache-reuse basin at higher latency.
Adaptive
post-\(Q2\) repair now gives no observed paired drift across 93 paired queries
at 3.20--6.51\,s repaired follow-up median and 15.28--35.97$\times$
all-query median speedup, while fixed \(K=1\) remains the no-coordination
baseline at 9.48--20.37$\times$ same-class follow-up speedup on the same
breadth. Scale-out operational rows under a separate 26B
protocol push the same idea further into streaming regimes with
4.2--4.5$\times$ real-video end-to-end speedups, double-digit ViT-call
reductions, and \mbox{$\sim$50$\times$} dominant measured subpipeline savings.
The Gemma 26B follow-up-latency probe is now a cautionary row rather than a
win: it observed fast warm responses, but the cross-turn cache-correctness
smoke failed, so correctness-preserving 26B C-PERSIST remains blocked.

The scientific value is in the boundaries as much as in the wins.
Fresh-compute placement over time cannot be reduced to novelty magnitude alone,
aggregate accuracy can hide answer-identity drift, and semantic substitution
must not be confused with measured sparse execution.

What remains is a fidelity-preserving measured sparse backend, broader benchmark coverage on the
second architecture, cleaner systems baselines, many-turn follow-up stress
tests, and direct cache-state instrumentation that explains why reused
prompt-cache state drifts. The n=60 composition audit, the long-bucket
admission sweep, and the cache-invalidated follow-up test already closed the
multiply-the-headlines temptation: the gains do not stack naively. The useful question now is
where each ceiling moves in the architectures and deployment protocols people
actually care about.

It also leaves a larger media agenda. The current paper is not an AI-native
codec and does not claim one. It shows why that direction is worth studying:
VLMs should not have to reverse-engineer every repeated frame back into state.
Future media streams for machines could expose change, motion, uncertainty,
objects, text, sensor time, and active tiles directly. Anti-recomputation is
one empirical step from video-as-frames toward video-as-world-state updates.
The strongest future version is not a better pixel threshold; it is a
model-facing media interface where change, motion, object state, sensor time,
and uncertainty arrive as first-class evidence.
