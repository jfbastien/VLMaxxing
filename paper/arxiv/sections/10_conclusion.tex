\section{Conclusion}

This paper argues for anti-recomputation, not for one particular trick. Fresh
videos admit modest but measured first-pass gains, and those gains obey a
share-limited ceiling that now binds on both Gemma headline cells and a
matched Qwen cross-architecture probe. Same-video follow-up queries admit much
larger multipliers, already reaching sub-second latency on the local stack, and
selective re-prefill now repairs the deeper cache-reuse basin: fixed \(K=1\)
gives no observed paired drift across 93 paired queries at 9.48--20.37$\times$
follow-up speedup, while the adaptive post-\(Q2\) state policy reaches
24.76$\times$ on the short slice. Streaming deployments push the same idea
further into operational regimes with double-digit to \mbox{$\sim$50$\times$}
savings. The routing and drift results explain why the bars differ so much:
fresh-compute placement over time matters more than novelty magnitude alone,
aggregate accuracy can hide answer-identity drift, and semantic substitution
must not be confused with measured sparse execution.

What remains is a measured sparse backend, broader benchmark coverage on the
second architecture, cleaner systems baselines, adaptive selective-reprefill
breadth, and a Qwen session bridge that either invalidates follow-up cache state
or directly explains why that state drifts. The n=60 composition null and the
1.30 long-bucket sweep already answered the easiest bad question: the gains do
not multiply naively. The useful question now is where each ceiling moves in
the architectures and deployment protocols people actually care about.
