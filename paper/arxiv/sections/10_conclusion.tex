\section{Conclusion}

This paper argues for anti-recomputation, not for one particular trick. Fresh
videos admit modest but measured first-pass gains, and those gains obey a
share-limited ceiling that now binds on both Gemma headline cells and a
matched Qwen cross-architecture probe. Same-video follow-up queries admit much
larger multipliers, already reaching sub-second latency on the local stack, and
the 20f selective-reprefill frontier now shows short+medium no-observed-drift
recovery at roughly 10$\times$ follow-up speedup. Streaming deployments
push the same idea further into operational regimes with double-digit to
\mbox{$\sim$50$\times$} savings. The routing and drift results explain why the
bars differ so much: fresh-compute placement over time matters more than
novelty magnitude alone, aggregate accuracy can hide answer-identity drift, and
semantic substitution must not be confused with measured sparse execution.

What remains is a measured sparse backend, broader benchmark coverage on the
second architecture, cleaner systems baselines, the long/depth boundary for
selective re-prefill, and a new Qwen session bridge policy rather than the
now-falsified \(kr_{Q0}=0.67\) candidate. The n=60 composition null already
answered the easiest bad question: the gains do not multiply naively. The
useful question now is where each ceiling moves in the architectures and
deployment protocols people actually care about.
