Video already carries evidence about what stayed still, what moved, and what is
new. Most video VLM stacks discard that structure, hand the model dense RGB
again, and ask it to rediscover state it could have reused. The factory wall
did not move, the stream has cheap evidence that it did not move, and the stack
still pays again for dense visual or prefix computation. We study that waste as
training-free anti-recomputation. The central question is where a runtime must
buy fresh visual evidence over time, and where cached state is enough to remain
on its regime-specific quality--compute frontier.

That question only makes sense if the accounting is honest. We separate two
local speed regimes and one imported deployment-scale regime: first-pass visual
work on fresh videos, after-ingest follow-up questions about the same video,
and streaming workloads that revisit nearly the same visual state. These are
not interchangeable. \emph{C-CEILING} formalizes the stage-share bound: a
component win becomes an end-to-end win only in proportion to the wall-clock
share it actually accelerates, explaining why a pooled n=60 composition attempt
is a ceiling-matched null rather than a multiplier.

The largest local gains appear after ingest. \emph{C-PERSIST} shows that
same-video follow-ups can reuse the already-paid prompt state, but also that
unrepaired reuse eventually enters a cache basin. Selective re-prefill refreshes
a small newest-frame visual tail and restores the tested follow-up contract:
fixed \(K=1\) shows no observed paired answer drift on 93 paired queries across
short, medium, long, and deeper VideoMME slices while retaining
9.48--20.37$\times$ same-class follow-up speedups, measured as cold follow-up
latency divided by repaired-session follow-up latency. A short-slice
state-aware adaptive cell reaches 24.76$\times$ same-class speedup with 0/21
paired drift.

Fresh-video pruning is smaller but measured. \emph{C-VISION} gives
1.113--1.407$\times$ first-pass wall-clock gains on Gemma 4-E4B-4bit across
clean and advisory holdout cells, and a matched Qwen point lands at
1.044$\times$ observed versus 1.043$\times$ predicted. The routing audits
explain why those numbers are not the whole story: fresh-compute placement
cannot be reduced to novelty magnitude alone, aggregate accuracy can hide
answer-identity drift, and semantic substitution is not a measured sparse
backend.

A separate 26B streaming protocol reports the same anti-recomputation structure
in operational regimes, including 13$\times$ fewer ViT calls,
\mbox{$\sim$50$\times$} dominant measured subpipeline reduction, and
4.2--4.5$\times$ end-to-end speedups. Those numbers are imported
deployment-scale evidence from a different protocol, not factors to multiply
with the local cells.

The broader implication is not that pixel differencing is the final interface.
The experiments are a requirements probe for future VLM-native media streams:
systems that expose change, motion, uncertainty, object state, sensor time, and
active tiles directly, so the model does not have to rediscover the world from
dense RGB every frame.
