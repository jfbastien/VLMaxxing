Video VLMs keep paying to understand pixels they have already understood, and
efficiency papers routinely overstate how much of a component-level win
survives to end-to-end wall-clock. We study this training-free
anti-recomputation problem across three non-interchangeable regimes:
first-pass vision pruning on fresh videos, after-ingest follow-up queries on
the same video, and routing under a fixed dense backend. First, we derive and
validate an arithmetic ceiling: stage-local speedups only survive in
proportion to the wall-clock share the accelerated stage actually owns; on
Gemma 4-E4B-4bit, the vision-axis analog explains first-pass gains from
1.113$\times$ to 1.407$\times$ across clean and advisory holdout cells and
correctly predicts the pooled n=60 composition null. Second, persistent-KV
reuse is the big-number regime: on Qwen2.5-VL-7B-4bit, same-video follow-up
questions drop to 815\,ms median at 47.2$\times$ speedup at 8 frames and
807\,ms at 91.1$\times$ at 16 frames inside the current 7B safe envelope,
with the 16f point clean and the 8f point still inside the current
safe-deployment criterion, while 3B
holds a wider but bounded $\Delta$acc $=-0.19$ plateau through 36 frames /
14.5k prefill tokens. Third, routing and deployment-scale streaming evidence
show why the numbers differ: fresh-compute placement matters more than novelty
magnitude alone, and the largest practical multipliers appear when the
expensive prefix or vision pass is being revisited. In companion
deployment-scale streaming experiments on a larger Gemma 26B stack, the same
thesis yields 13$\times$ fewer ViT calls,
\mbox{$\sim$50$\times$} dominant-pipeline reduction, and 4.2--4.5$\times$
end-to-end speedups in real-video regimes.
