Video VLMs keep paying to understand pixels they have already understood. The
factory wall did not move, the stream has cheap evidence that it did not move,
and the stack still pays again for dense visual or prefix computation. We study
that waste as training-free anti-recomputation, while keeping the accounting
honest: component-level efficiency wins are easy to misread as end-to-end
wall-clock wins unless the accelerated stage's share is reported. We measure
three places where repeated work appears: the first pass over a fresh video,
later questions about a video that has already been ingested, and
streaming/deployment settings. The regimes have different ceilings.
\emph{C-CEILING} formalizes the stage-share bound and explains why the pooled
n=60 composition attempt is a null rather than a multiplier.
\emph{C-PERSIST} is the largest observed follow-up-speedup regime: same-video
follow-ups can reuse the already-paid prompt state, but unrepaired reuse
eventually enters a cache basin. Selective re-prefill repairs that basin by refreshing a small
newest-frame visual tail. A fixed repair policy shows no observed paired answer
drift on 93 paired queries across short, medium, long, and deeper VideoMME
slices, with 9.48--20.37$\times$ same-class follow-up speedups, defined as
median cold follow-up latency divided by median repaired-session follow-up
latency. A state-aware adaptive policy is the strongest single cell, reaching
24.76$\times$ same-class speedup with 0/21 paired drift.
\emph{C-VISION} shows that first-pass vision pruning gives modest but measured
wall-clock gains: 1.113$\times$ to 1.407$\times$ on Gemma 4-E4B-4bit across
clean and advisory holdout cells, with a matched Qwen point landing at
1.044$\times$ observed versus 1.043$\times$ predicted. Routing and paired-drift
audits explain the boundaries: fresh-compute placement cannot be reduced to
novelty magnitude alone, aggregate accuracy can hide answer-identity drift, and
semantic substitution is not a measured sparse backend. A separate 26B
streaming protocol reports the same anti-recomputation structure in operational
regimes, including 13$\times$ fewer ViT calls, \mbox{$\sim$50$\times$} dominant
measured subpipeline reduction, and 4.2--4.5$\times$ end-to-end speedups. These
numbers are different regimes, not one speedup curve, and they should not be
multiplied.
The broader implication is not that pixel differencing is the final interface:
the experiments are a requirements probe for future VLM-native media streams
that expose change, motion, uncertainty, and state directly while keeping the
present paper's claims limited to measured evidence.
