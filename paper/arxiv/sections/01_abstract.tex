Video VLMs keep paying to understand pixels they have already understood, and
component-level efficiency wins are easy to misread as end-to-end wall-clock
wins unless the accelerated stage's share is reported. We study training-free
anti-recomputation across three places where this waste appears: the first
pass over a fresh video, later questions about a video that has already been
ingested, and streaming/deployment settings. The regimes have different
ceilings. \emph{C-CEILING} formalizes the stage-share bound and explains why
the pooled n=60 composition attempt is a null rather than a multiplier.
\emph{C-VISION} shows that first-pass vision pruning gives modest but measured
wall-clock gains: 1.113$\times$ to 1.407$\times$ on Gemma 4-E4B-4bit across
clean and advisory holdout cells, with a matched Qwen point landing at
1.044$\times$ observed versus 1.043$\times$ predicted. The big-number regime
is \emph{C-PERSIST}: same-video follow-ups can reuse the already-paid prompt
state, but unrepaired reuse eventually enters a cache basin. Selective
re-prefill repairs that basin by refreshing a small newest-frame visual tail.
A fixed repair policy shows no observed paired answer drift on 93 paired
queries across short, medium, long, and deeper VideoMME slices, with
9.48--20.37$\times$ follow-up speedups. A state-aware adaptive policy is the
strongest single cell, reaching 24.76$\times$ speedup with 0/21 paired drift.
Routing and paired-drift audits explain the boundaries: fresh-compute
placement matters more than novelty magnitude alone, aggregate accuracy can
hide answer-identity drift, and semantic substitution is not a measured sparse
backend. Companion deployment-scale experiments on a larger Gemma 26B stack
report the same anti-recomputation structure in operational regimes, including
13$\times$ fewer ViT calls, \mbox{$\sim$50$\times$} dominant-pipeline
reduction, and 4.2--4.5$\times$ end-to-end speedups. These numbers are
different regimes, not one speedup curve, and they should not be multiplied.
