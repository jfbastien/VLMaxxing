\section{Introduction}

Video VLMs spend a surprising amount of time rediscovering what they already
know. A benchmark clip is sampled into several frames, many of those frames are
visually redundant, and the stack still pays again for vision encoding,
prefill, or both. The same waste appears in repeated follow-up questions on the
same video, and it becomes even more obvious in live streams where most of the
scene remains stable for long stretches. The interesting question is no longer
whether redundancy exists. It does. The question is where that redundancy can
be removed without breaking the answer.

The literature often treats that waste as if it were one knob. It is not. The
fresh-video pass is bounded by whatever share of wall-clock the vision tower
actually owns. After-ingest follow-up queries on the same video are a
different regime: once the prefix has been paid, prompt reuse can dominate.
Routing under a fixed dense backend is different again: it tells us whether a
training-free policy preserves answer quality, not whether the backend has
already been rewritten to skip timed work. Reuse is three regimes with three
different ceilings, not one multiplier that can be copied from paper to paper.

That distinction changes what the paper is about. The first contribution is
\emph{C-CEILING}: an arithmetic bound on how much of a local speedup can
survive to end-to-end wall-clock. The second is \emph{C-PERSIST}: the
after-ingest same-video regime where the biggest local multipliers already
exist. The third is \emph{C-VISION}: measured first-pass gains on fresh videos
via training-free vision-tower pruning. Qwen routing then plays a different
role: it is the mechanism-validation backbone and the negative-result ledger
that explains why fresh-compute placement matters more than novelty magnitude
alone.

The story also survives contact with operational workloads. In companion
deployment-scale experiments on a larger Gemma 4 26B streaming stack,
same-video follow-up latency is 0.8\,s, paired Streaming VideoMME reports
13$\times$ fewer ViT calls and
\mbox{$\sim$50$\times$} less dominant-pipeline compute, and selected real-video
regimes reach 4.2--4.5$\times$ end-to-end speedups. Those numbers come from a
different protocol and stay labeled that way in the paper. They matter because
they show that the same reuse structure survives operational settings.

We make three contributions:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
\item We derive and validate \textbf{C-CEILING}: a stage-share ceiling for
end-to-end gain. The same arithmetic explains both the modest first-pass
multipliers and the pooled n=60 composition null, where the observed
1.042$\times$ stacked result lands almost exactly where the ceiling predicts.
\item We characterize \textbf{C-PERSIST}: after-ingest persistent-KV reuse on
Qwen collapses follow-up latency to roughly conversational timescales, with a
7B safe envelope through 16 frames / 6.5k prefill tokens, a clean 16f point,
and a wider but bounded 3B plateau through 36 frames / 14.5k prefill tokens.
\item We establish \textbf{C-VISION}: training-free mid-layer vision-tower
pruning yields measured first-pass end-to-end gains on frozen Gemma video-VLM
runs, from 1.113$\times$ to 1.407$\times$ across clean and advisory holdout
cells, with magnitude governed by the vision-share ceiling rather than wishful
component-wise multiplication.
\end{itemize}

Those three contributions do not compose as a naive product. C-VISION changes
first-pass latency. C-PERSIST changes follow-up latency on later queries about
the same video. C-CEILING says shared stage budgets cannot be spent twice. The
paper therefore reports composition against measured ceilings, not against
headline multiplication.

Qwen routing and its preserved nulls are what keep that framing honest. The
holdout planner results show a real quality--compute frontier under a frozen
dense backend, novelty-ranked dense baselines show that quantity alone is the
wrong variable, and the VideoMME frame-count probes show that ``more frames''
is not a linear quality knob on the local Qwen stack. The dev-split 16-frame
long-bucket regression does \emph{not} replicate on disjoint holdout; that is
exactly the kind of boundary the paper keeps instead of smoothing away.
