\section{Introduction}

Video VLMs spend a surprising amount of time rediscovering what they already
know. The wall behind a factory robot did not move. The stove, countertop, or
security-camera background often did not move either. A vehicle's front camera
also sees much of the world move predictably: nearby objects expand, lane
markers flow outward, and the surprise is concentrated near boundaries,
occlusions, and independent movers. The video stream carries cheap evidence of
stasis, motion, and residual surprise, and classical codecs have spent decades
turning that evidence into compact state. Yet most VLM pipelines hand the model
another dense stack of RGB frames and ask it to pay again for vision encoding,
prefill, or both.

That repeated payment is the seed of this paper. A benchmark clip is sampled
into several frames, many of those frames are visually redundant, and the stack
still treats them as fresh. The same waste appears in repeated follow-up
questions on the same video, and it becomes even more obvious in live streams
where most of the scene remains stable for long stretches. The interesting
question is no longer whether redundancy exists. It does. The question is
where that redundancy can be removed without breaking the answer.

Our central question is where a VLM must buy fresh visual evidence over time,
and where cached state is enough to stay on the relevant regime-specific
quality--compute frontier. The key variable is not simply how much visual
compute is reused. A small transient event, a moving hand, a text flash, or an
object entering at a boundary can matter more than a large region of
predictable background motion. The planner buys fresh evidence; the model
still decides what matters.

The literature often treats that waste as if it were one knob. It is not. The
fresh-video pass is bounded by whatever share of wall-clock the vision tower
actually owns. After-ingest follow-up queries on the same video are a
different regime: once the prefix has been paid, prompt reuse can dominate.
Routing under a fixed dense backend is different again: it tells us whether a
training-free policy preserves answer quality, not whether the backend has
already been rewritten to skip timed work. Reuse is three regimes with three
different ceilings, not one multiplier that can be copied from paper to paper.

That distinction changes what the paper is about. The first contribution is
\emph{stage-share ceiling (C-CEILING)}: an arithmetic bound on how much of a
local speedup can survive to end-to-end wall-clock. The second is
\emph{persistent follow-up reuse (C-PERSIST)}: the after-ingest same-video
regime where the biggest local multipliers already exist, but also where cache
reuse enters a measurable basin unless the visual tail is repaired. The third
is \emph{first-pass vision pruning (C-VISION)}: measured first-pass gains on
fresh videos via training-free vision-tower pruning. Qwen routing then plays a
different role: it is the mechanism-validation backbone and the negative-result
ledger; local novelty-ranked diagnostics suggest that fresh-compute placement
is not captured by novelty magnitude alone, but those diagnostics remain
outside the release claim surface until their raw outputs are checked in.

The larger target is not a bigger threshold sweep and not a new codec in this
paper. The current experiments are a measurement instrument: they ask which
cheap temporal signals are already useful to a frozen VLM runtime, where those
signals fail, and what a future VLM-native media stream should expose directly.
Codec and pixel signals are therefore treated as freshness and uncertainty
signals, not as semantic-saliency oracles. They can say where something
probably changed; the model and the task still decide what matters. That is
why the VLM-native media discussion later in the paper is framed as candidate
requirements, not as a current systems claim.

The same boundary applies to training. These are training-free inference
experiments, not measured training-speedup experiments. The training-relevant
claim is narrower and more useful: the traces identify where frozen models
already tolerate reuse, where they do not, and what a future model could learn
as native refresh gates, delta encoders, or cache-state-aware attention.

The scale-out streaming lane is part of the same science story, but it has a
different protocol and is not yet a release claim. The intended end-state is a
first-class \emph{streaming state reuse} evaluation with the same claim axes,
artifact format, paired gates, cache-correctness smokes, and baselines as the
local runs. Until that validated bundle lands, candidate C-STREAM is motivation
and a set of gates, not a multiplier to inherit. This caution is earned by our
Qwen session-composition experiments: they reproduce speedup without preserving
the full fidelity contract, and later sweeps rule out the tested prompt-cache
family as a deployable composition policy. Even when the first answer is
preserved, reused cache state can still damage later answers.

The table is a workload map. The contributions below are the analytical and
empirical pieces that explain those workloads without collapsing them into a
single multiplier. We make three contributions:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
\item We derive and validate \textbf{stage-share ceiling (C-CEILING)}: a stage-share ceiling for
end-to-end gain. The same arithmetic explains both the modest first-pass
multipliers and the n=60 composition audit, where the observed
1.042$\times$ stacked result lands almost exactly where the measured share
terms predict.
\item We characterize \textbf{persistent follow-up reuse (C-PERSIST)}: after-ingest persistent-KV reuse on
Qwen collapses follow-up latency to roughly conversational timescales, with a
7B tested envelope through 16 frames / 6.5k prefill tokens, a clean 16f point,
and a wider but bounded 3B plateau through 36 frames / 14.5k prefill tokens.
We then show that the 7B basin is repairable: selective re-prefill of a fixed
newest-frame visual tail gives no observed paired choice or correctness drift
on 93 paired queries spanning short, medium, long, and 32-frame short slices,
while retaining 9.48--20.37$\times$ follow-up speedup. A state-aware adaptive
variant repairs once and lets the next follow-up inherit that repaired state;
across the same 93-query breadth, it has no observed paired drift and reaches
14.90--35.92$\times$ same-class follow-up speedup
(15.28--35.97$\times$ cold-all-query / repaired-follow-up ratio).
\item We establish \textbf{first-pass vision pruning (C-VISION)}: training-free mid-layer vision-tower
pruning yields measured first-pass end-to-end gains on frozen Gemma video-VLM
runs, from a clean 1.113$\times$ VideoMME holdout to advisory larger cells,
with magnitude governed by the vision-share ceiling rather than wishful
component-wise multiplication. The measured sparse-execution envelope then
separates systems truth from fidelity truth: Gemma 32f short is a clean
1.316$\times$ timed-skip cell, while Qwen needs a conservative low-gain keep
rate to restore fidelity.
\end{itemize}

Those three contributions do not compose as a naive product. C-VISION changes
first-pass latency. C-PERSIST changes follow-up latency on later queries about
the same video. C-CEILING says shared stage budgets cannot be spent twice. The
paper therefore reports composition against measured ceilings, not against
headline multiplication.

Qwen routing and its preserved nulls are what keep that framing honest. The
holdout planner results show a real quality--compute frontier under a frozen
dense backend, local novelty-ranked dense diagnostics suggest that quantity
alone is the wrong variable, and the VideoMME frame-count probes show that
``more frames'' is not a linear quality knob on the Qwen stack. The dev-split
16-frame
long-bucket regression does \emph{not} replicate on disjoint holdout; that is
exactly the kind of boundary the paper keeps instead of smoothing away.
The same discipline now applies to paired drift: several runs preserve
aggregate accuracy while changing individual answers, so the paper reports
choice diffs, correctness diffs, pathological-attractor counts, and confidence
intervals whenever they decide the claim boundary.
